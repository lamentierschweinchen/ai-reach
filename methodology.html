Large Labor Model
WHEN WILL AI REACH YOUR JOB?

The model tracks two things: labor shares — how many people work in each territory, globally — and replaceability — when AI or robotics becomes technically capable of performing the core tasks of each territory, scored 0 to 100.

These are separate measurements. Replaceability tracks the technical frontier: can the best commercially available AI or robotics do this job? A prototype in a lab does not count. A product you can buy does. Regulation, economics, and social preference do not affect the score — they are recorded separately. This means a territory can score 90 while employment has barely changed. That gap between what AI can do and what has actually happened to jobs is not a flaw in the model. It is one of the most important things it shows.

Labor data from 1800 to 1870 is reconstructed from Bairoch (1988), Mitchell (2003), and Cambridge Group occupational records. From 1991 onward, data uses ILO modelled estimates at the ISIC Rev. 4 section level across 189 countries. The 13 territories are an editorial grouping over these standard classifications.

From 2027 onward, projected employment is derived from a displacement model. It starts from an ILO consensus baseline — where employment would go if AI displacement did not occur — and applies two forces. New replaceability gains after 2026 are converted into displacement at a rate of one-third: each new point of AI capability costs roughly one-third of a percentage point of that territory's workforce. Existing capability that has not yet translated into job losses — the gap visible in 2026 between what AI can do and how many people still do the work — is absorbed over 15 years on a decelerating curve. These two parameters are the model's most consequential assumptions and the most appropriate targets for challenge.

The dataset was produced through a multi-model adversarial pipeline — independent AI research instances producing scores without seeing each other's work, reconciled by a third instance, and validated by a final editorial layer. Manual review by the project leads covered structural decisions and spot-checking. Every number in the dataset should be explainable; where it isn't, that is a known limitation documented in the full methodology.

The model is deliberately reductive and intentionally incomplete. It does not model job creation, policy responses, or economic restructuring. It shows the displacement pressure. What happens next is a political question, not a technical one.

Large Labor Model traces human labor across two and a half centuries, from 1800 to 2041. While every prior transformation of work displaced old categories and created new ones, this piece examines whether that pattern continues, or whether AI and autonomous systems are arriving not to reshape work but to redefine it.

Where this question is discussed, it is often simplified. Labor is not only economic but entangled with identity, worth, and belonging. Technology has disrupted work before, but never with this speed, reach, and scale.

Thirteen territories group human labor into deliberately crude shapes — modeled in collaboration with the very systems the project examines. The data is provisional, the methodology is open, and the work is unfinished by design. It does not predict the future. It asks questions of it.
