Large Labour Model
WHEN WILL AI REACH YOUR JOB?

The model tracks two things: labor shares — how many people work in each territory, globally — and replaceability — when AI or robotics becomes technically capable of performing the core tasks of each territory, scored 0 to 100.

These are separate measurements. Replaceability tracks the technical frontier: can the best commercially available AI or robotics do this job? A prototype in a lab does not count. A product you can buy does. Regulation, economics, and social preference do not affect the score — they are recorded separately. This means a territory can score 90 while employment has barely changed. That gap between what AI can do and what has actually happened to jobs is not a flaw in the model. It is one of the most important things it shows.

Labor data from 1800 to 1870 is reconstructed from Bairoch (1988), Mitchell (2003), and Cambridge Group occupational records. From 1991 onward, data uses ILO modelled estimates at the ISIC Rev. 4 section level across 189 countries. The 13 territories are an editorial grouping over these standard classifications.

Projections from 2027 to 2041 were computed under three scenarios — conservative, moderate, and accelerated. The visualisation shows the moderate scenario only. The conservative scenario serves as the baseline: where employment would go if AI displacement did not occur. The displacement model converts replaceability gains into projected job losses using a conversion rate (one-third of each new replaceability point becomes displacement) and a lag schedule (existing capability is absorbed over 15 years on a decelerating curve). These are the model's most consequential assumptions and the most appropriate targets for challenge.

The dataset was produced through a multi-model adversarial pipeline — independent AI research instances producing scores without seeing each other's work, reconciled by a third instance, and validated by Claude (Opus 4.6) as final editor. Manual review by the project lead covered structural decisions and spot-checking. Every number in the dataset should be explainable; where it isn't, that is a known limitation documented in the full methodology.

The model is deliberately reductive and intentionally incomplete. It does not model job creation, policy responses, or economic restructuring. It shows the displacement pressure. What happens next is a political question, not a technical one.

Large Labour Model is a data visualisation that maps 240 years of human work — and projects where AI and robotics are taking it next. It is designed to be explored, not passively consumed: the viewer controls time, searches for their own profession, and watches sectors emerge, grow, and come under pressure.

The visual language borrows from Matisse's cut-outs — bold, flat shapes with imperfect edges, layered without strict alignment. Each territory of work is a coloured form whose area reflects its share of the global workforce. As the timeline advances, shapes shift, new sectors emerge from larger ones, and replaceability percentages begin to appear. The aesthetic is intentional: the warmth and texture of the forms sit in tension with the coldness of what the numbers describe.

The piece does not advocate for a position on whether automation is good or bad. It presents a trend line and invites an emotional response. The question it asks is not "what should we do?" but "what do you feel when you see this?"
